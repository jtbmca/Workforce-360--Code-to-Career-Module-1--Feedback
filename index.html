Module 1-1: Workforce 360: Code to Career - Digital Foundations & Cybersecurity - Digital Literacy & Safe Practices: Introduction & Digital Literacy

“Will the video open in a new tab, and you will need to go back to the open class tab in your browser?”

This sentence appears to be written by a displaced time traveler, and as such, is from a future where causal relations affect English differently and new verb tenses have been invented. Of course, this could be easily fixed by changing the order of “you” and “will”.

Instead, I think the question would be clearer if it read, “True or False: After viewing a video in a new tab, must you return to the MCA Open Class Tab in your browser?” and change the answers accordingly.

Furthermore, the lesson portion should read, “The video will open in a new tab. Once the video is complete, you must continue return to the MCA Open Class tab”, regardless of which approach is taken as the learner is not actually “continuing” down that branch of research, it ends when the video closes.

"To the tab, back we must go?"

Module 1-5: Professional Communication Skills Lesson

This module is by far the most flawed. Primarily this is due to contradictory information provided to the learner by two of its key video components. Secondarily, it also fails by providing a low-quality video example.

While both of the presenters are excellent at their jobs, they do provide slight differences of opinion on certain etiquettes. For instance, both channels agree that one subject per email is appropriate, however they differ on approach when it comes to follow-up emails. The speaker from Harvard Business Review (who I find to be an exceptional source) suggests that similar subject replies should stay in the same thread as the original subject, while the second presenter prefers to create new ones for the sake of clarity.

"different keystrokes"

Using Adriana Girdler's approach could cause a learner to fail the quiz on this subject, even if the written lesson explicitly states that "hey" is not acceptable. In today's vernacular, (regardless of whether boomers or Gen X agree,) "Hello", "Hi", and "Hey" are all interchangeable. At work and personally, I receive many sales and marketing emails that begin with "hey". While Girdler only says "hello", or "hi", it is implied that being less formal is better, as we will see in the next example. if a student were to take her advice to heart, they may very well steer away from the "dear [name]" answer and towards the imperfect "hey [name]" answer as it is closer to what they perceive to be correct.

"Hey, is it me you're looking for?"

When she warns against saying, "to whom it may concern", she is inadvertently calling into question all other greetings perceived as "formal" Moreover, her examples stick to the "less formal" side of business speak. One of the her most strict closings is, "warmest regards", which to some would line up with "cheers' more than an answer that contains the more formal, "Sincerely". I think the chance of this being misunderstood is higher for the neurodivergent, the youth, and people who speak English as a second language. recommendations: find another video example or relax style recommendation slightly to reflect the modern landscape

While this video is on-topic and supplements the material well (especially the last paragraph of the written lesson) it is of poor quality with a disgusting ai-voice and graphics that are little more than a powerpoint; the medium is not being utilized properly
